Intro to my Thesis Research

18 February, 2022

My thesis research belongs to arithmetic dynamics, a relatively new subdiscipline of pure mathematics in which we study dynamical systems from the point of view of number theory. In writing this note, I have tried to assume an absolute minimum of mathematical knowledge. My hope is that anyone who reads the first two sections ("Dynamical Systems and Periodic Points", and "Motivating Problem") will come away with at least some sense of the flavor of my thesis research. Beginning in the third section ("Dynatomic Polynomials and their Galois Groups"), I will assume the reader is familiar with the language of Galois groups, function fields, and curves. Despite having meditated on the issue, I don't think it's possible to give a precise statement of the main theorem of my thesis without this fancier language.

Dynamical Systems and Periodic Points

For us, a dynamical system is any function \( f: \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Q} \). (Recall that \( \mathbb{Q} \) denotes the set of all rational numbers). Given such a dynamical system, we can talk about iterating the function \( f \), by which we mean composing \( f \) with itself some number of times: for any element \( a \) of \( \mathbb{Q} \) , the output \( f(a) \) is also an element of \( \mathbb{Q} \), so we can apply \( f \) again in order to obtain \( f^2 (a) := f(f(a)) \), and so on. For a rational number \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \), we will let \( f_c (x) \) denote the quadratic polynomial \( f_c (x):= x^2 + c \).

Example:

  • Let \( c = 0 \in \mathbb{Q} \), so that \( f_c (x) = x^2 +0 = x^2 \) . Then \( f_c \) is a dynamical system. To illustrate iteration of our function \( f_c \) we can start from any element, say \( a = 2 \in \mathbb{Q} \), and compute: $$\begin{aligned} f_c^1& (2) = f_c (2) = (2)^2 = 4 \\[0.5em] f_c^2& (2) = f_c (f_c(2)) = f_c (4) = (4)^2 = 16 \\[0.5em] f_c^3& (2) = f_c (f_c^2 (2)) = f_c (16) = (16)^2 = 256 \\ &\vdots \end{aligned}$$

Now let \( f: \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Q} \) be an arithmetic dynamical system. An element \( a \in \mathbb{Q} \) is said to be a periodic point for \( f \) if there is some number \( k \gt 0 \) for which \[ f^k (a) = a. \] In other words, if we start from \( a \) and apply \( f \) to it \( k \)-many times, we get \( a \) back. For a periodic point \( a \in \mathbb{Q} \), the period of \( a \) is the smallest number \( n \gt 0 \) for which \( f^n (a) = a \). (We will equivalently say that \( a \) is an \( n \)-periodic point for \( f \) ).

Examples:

  • Let \( c = 0 \), so that \( f_c (x) = x^2 +0 = x^2 \). Then the calculations \[ f_c(0) = (0)^2 = 0, \ \ f_c(1) = (1)^2 = 1 \] show that each of \( 0 \) and \( 1 \) is a \( 1 \)-periodic point for \( f_c \). (It is also common to call a \( 1 \)-periodic point a fixed point).

  • Let \( c = -1 \). Then \( f_c (x) = x^2 + (-1) = x^2 -1 \) is a dynamical system, and the calculations $$\begin{aligned} f_c (0) &= (0)^2 -1 = -1, \\[0.5em] f_c^2 (0) &= f_c (f_c (0)) \\[0.5em] &= f_c (-1) \\[0.5em] &= (-1)^2 -1 \\[0.5em] &= 1 -1 = 0 \end{aligned}$$ show that each of \( 0 \) and \( -1 \) is a \( 2 \)-periodic point for \( f_c \).

  • Let \( c = \frac{-29}{16} \). Then \( f_c (x) = x^2 + \left( \frac{-29}{16} \right) = x^2 - \frac{29}{16} \) is a dynamical system. Calculations similar to those above show that each of \( \frac{-1}{4} \), \( \frac{-7}{4} \), and \( \frac{5}{4} \) is a \( 3 \)-periodic point for \( f_c \). (Start from any of these three values, say \( a = \frac{-1}{4} \) and then compute \( f_c (a) \), \( f_c ^2 (a) \), and \( f_c^3 (a) \)).

Motivating Problem

To recap, above we saw explicit examples of quadratic polynomials \( f_c(x) = x^2 + c \) which have \( 1 \)-periodic, \( 2 \)-periodic, and \( 3 \)-periodic points. Great! But now one might naturally ask: "does this keep going? Are there examples of quadratics \( f_c \) with points of period \( 4 \), \( 5 \), \( 6 \), and so on?" This is the central question of my thesis research, and the best answer is: we think there are no such examples, but we're not sure.

Conjecture: (Flynn, Poonen, Schaefer---1995)

If \( n \) is at least \( 4 \), then there is no \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \) such that the quadratic polynomial \( f_c (x) = x^2 + c \) has a point of period \( n \) in \( \mathbb{Q} \).

(Recall that in mathematics, a conjecture is a statement that is strongly believed to be true, but which has yet to be formally proved).

We will henceforth use the shorthand FPS to refer to the Conjecture of Flynn, Poonen, and Schaefer above. While FPS has yet to be proved in general, we do have proofs in a few small cases:

  • FPS holds for the case \( n=4 \) (Morton---1992);
  • FPS holds for the case \( n = 5 \) (Flynn, Poonen, Schaefer---1995);
  • if the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture is true, then FPS holds for the case \( n=6 \) (Stoll---2008)

Let's now assume the role of "mathematical devil's advocate" as we discuss how FPS might fail to be true. To show that FPS is false, it is enough to find a single counterexample. That is, it is enough to find a single number \( n \ge 4 \) and a single rational value \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \) such that \( f_c(x) \) has a point of period \( n \) in \( \mathbb{Q} \). If someone found a single such counterexample, it would be a sad day for FPS supporters, but it could be much worse. Indeed, suppose someone found some integer \( n \ge 4 \) and infinitely many rational values \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \) such that each quadratic \( f_c \) has a point of period \( n \) in \( \mathbb{Q} \). Then not only would FPS be false, but it would be false infinitely many times!

For each integer \( n \ge 1 \), let \( \mathbf{\mathrm{F.P.}(n)} \) denote the statement: "there are at most finitely many \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \) such that \( f_c (x) = x^2 + c \) has a point of period \( n \) in \( \mathbb{Q} \)." We have the following:

Theorem: (Morton---1996)

\( \mathrm{F.P.} (n) \) holds for every \( n \ge 4 \).

One consequence of my thesis research is that \( \mathrm{F.P.}(n) \) holds for every \( n \ge 10 \). But as we see in the previous Theorem, this was already known. For a mathematician to earn a Ph.D. they must prove something new. What then is that "something new" which I proved in my thesis? To answer this, we will require some fancier language.

Periodic Points More Generally

In the previous two sections, I have tried to assume zero mathematical background. Unfortunately, I don't think it's possible to have an efficient and precise discussion of the results of my thesis without assuming some familiarity with Galois groups, functions fields, and curves.

Going forward, we will need a more general framework for discussing periodic points:

  • let \( \mathbb{F} \) be any perfect field and let \( \bar{\mathbb{F}} \) be a fixed algebraic closure of \( \mathbb{F} \);
  • let \( g(x) \in \mathbb{F}[x] \) be any non-constant polynomial;
  • the set \( \mathrm{Per}(g) \) of all periodic points for \( g \) is defined by \[ \mathrm{Per}(g):= \{ \alpha \in \bar{\mathbb{F}} : g^k (\alpha) = \alpha \text{, some \( k \ge 1 \) } \}; \]
  • the period of \( \alpha \in \mathrm{Per}(g) \) is the positive integer \[ n:= \mathrm{min} \{ k \ge 1 : g^k (\alpha) = \alpha \}, \] and we will equivalently say that \( \alpha \) is \( n \)-periodic for \( g \). Let \[ \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (g) := \{ \alpha \in \bar{\mathbb{F}} : \text{\( \alpha \) is \( n \)-periodic for \( g \)} \} \] denote the set of all \( n \)-periodic points for \( g \). Finally, given any subset \( S \subseteq \bar{\mathbb{F}} \), we will let \[ \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (g; S) := \mathrm{Per} (g) \cap S \] denote the set of all \( n \)-periodic points in \( S \) .

Dynatomic Polynomials and their Galois Groups

Now, let \( t \) be transcendental over \( \mathbb{C} \), let \( K \) denote the rational function field \( K := \mathbb{Q}(t) \), let \( A \) denote the polynomial ring \( A:= \mathbb{Q}[t] \), and let \( f(x) \) denote the (generic) quadratic polynomial \( f(x):= x^2 +t \in A[x] \). In order to study the dynamics of the quadratic polynomial \( f_c (x) = x^2 + c \in \mathbb{Q}[x] \) for each \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \), we will instead study the dynamics of \( f(x) \), and how these dynamics behave under "specialization."

Fix an integer \( n \ge 1 \). Our key tool for studying the \( n \)-periodic points of \( f(x) \) is the \( n \)-th dynatomic polynomial : \[ \Phi_{n } \left( x \right) := \prod_{d \mid n} \left( f^d(x) - x \right)^{\mu(n/d)} \] where \( \mu \) is the Möbius function. The utility of \( \Phi_{n } (x) \) is that its zeros are precisely the \( n \)-periodic points of \( f \). Indeed, let \( L_n \) denote the splitting field of \( \Phi_{n } \) over \( K \) , and let \[ Z_n := \{ \alpha \in L_n : \Phi_n (\alpha) = 0 \} \] denote the (geometric) zero set of \( \Phi_n (x) \) . Then the following hold:

  1. \( \Phi_{n } \left( x \right) \in A[x] \) and \( \Phi_{n } \left( x \right) \) is monic in \( x \);
  2. \( \Phi_{n } \left( x \right) \) is irreducible over \( \mathbb{C} (t) \);
  3. \( Z_n = \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (f) \);
  4. \( \Phi_{n } (x) \) divides \( \Phi_{n } \left( f(x) \right) \).
(See Proposition 1.3.3.) A consequence of c. and d. above is that \( n \) divides \( \deg \Phi_{n } (x) \); let \( r \) denote the integer \[ r:= \frac{\deg \Phi_{n } \left( x \right)}{n}. \]

Our quadratic polynomial \( f(x) = x^2 + t \in K[x] \) is a "generic representative" for each quadratic \( f_c (x) = x^2 + c \in \mathbb{Q}[x] \) in the sense that every \( f_c \) is obtained from \( f \) by specialization. That is, for each \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \) we have that \[ f(x) \mid_{t = c} = (x^2 +t)|_{t = c} = x^2 +c = f_c (x). \] This "generic representative" philosophy also extends to dynatomic polynomials. Indeed, fix some \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \). Then \[ \Phi_{n,c} (x) := \prod_{d \mid n} \left( f_c^d (x) - x \right) ^{\mu(n/d)} \in \mathbb{Q} [x] \] is the \( n \)-th dynatomic polynomial (specialized) at \( c \) . Notice that \[ \Phi_{n ,c } \left( x \right) = \Phi_{n } \left( x \right) |_{t = c} \] so that \( \Phi_{n,c} \) deserves its name.

We will let \( L_{n,c} \) denote the splitting field of \( \Phi_{n ,c } \) over \( \mathbb{Q} \) , and \[ Z_{n,c}:= \{ \alpha \in L_{n,c} : \Phi_{n ,c } \left( \alpha \right) = 0\} \] will denote the (geometric) zero set of \( \Phi_{n ,c } (x) \) . The containment \( \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (f_c) \subseteq Z_{n,c} \) still holds, but unlike in the generic setting (where we saw that \( Z_n = \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (f) \)), we don't always have equality.

Now, each of the field extensions \( L_n / K \) and \( L_{n,c} / \mathbb{Q} \) is Galois, so we can discuss their Galois groups: \[ G_n:= \mathrm{Gal}(L_n/K) \] is the \( n \)-th dynatomic Galois group , and \[ G_{n,c}:= \mathrm{Gal}(L_{n,c} /\mathbb{Q} ) \] is the \( n \)-th dynatomic Galois group (specialized) at \( c \) . By the work (Bousch---1992), we have an explicit description of \( G_n \) as a semi-direct product: \[ G_n \cong \left( \mathbb{Z} / n \right)^{ r } \rtimes S_{ r }, \] where \( S_r \) acts by automorphisms on \( \left( \mathbb{Z} / n \right)^{ r } \) by permuting factors according to the natural action of \( S_r \) on \( [r]:=\{1 , \ldots, r\} \). This result begs the question: do we have a similarly nice description of the specialized dynatomic Galois groups \( G_{n,c} \)? If we define \[ D_n := \{ c \in \mathbb{Q} : \text{\( \Phi_{n ,c } (x) \) is inseparable} \}, \] then we can give a partial answer:

Proposition: (See Corollary 2.3.7.)

If \( c \in \mathbb{Q} -D_n \), then \( G_{n,c} \le G_n \).

Since \( D_n \) is the zero set of the discriminant of \( \Phi_{n } \left( x \right) \), we know that \( D_n \) is finite. Thus, outside of a finite set \( D_n \) of values, \( G_{n,c} \) embeds as a subgroup of \( G_n \).

But knowing that \( G_{n,c} \le G_n \) may not be super helpful by itself. As we can see in the table $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|} \hline n & \left| G_n \right| &n & \left| G_n \right| \\ \hline 1 & 2 & 5 & 11250000 \\ \hline 2 & 2 & 6 & \sim 10^{12} \\ \hline 3 & 18 & 7 & \sim 10^{31} \\ \hline 4 & 384 & 8 & \sim 10^{59} \\ \hline \end{array}$$ the size \( \left| G_n \right| \) appears to grow exponentially with \( n \) so that the size of the subgroup lattice of \( G_n \) becomes incomprehensibly large very quickly. That said, there is one (non-trivial) subgroup of \( G_n \) whose structure we understand: the full subgroup \( G_n \) itself! We may then ask: how often is it the case that \( G_{n,c} = G_n \)? The main Theorem of my thesis gives an answer: if \( n \ge 10 \), then \( G_{n,c} = G_n \) almost always.

Exceptional Values and Periodic Points

Let \[ E_n := \{ c \in \mathbb{Q} - D_n : G_{n,c} \lneq G_n \} \] be the set of all values \( c \in \mathbb{Q} -D_n \) for which \( G_{n,c} \) embeds as a proper subgroup of \( G_n \). We call \( E_n \) the \( n \)-th exceptional set , and elements of \( E_n \) are called exceptional values . We are finally equipped to make a connection between dynatomic Galois groups and periodic points in \( \mathbb{Q} \) for quadratic polynomials:

Theorem: (Theorem 2.3.8.)

Let \( c \in \mathbb{Q} -D_n \). If \( \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (f_c; \mathbb{ Q } ) \) is non-empty, then \( c \in E_n \).

Recall from earlier that \( \mathrm{F.P.}(n) \) is a short hand for the statement: "there are at most finitely many \( c \in \mathbb{Q} \) for which \( \mathrm{Per}_{ n } (f_c; \mathbb{ Q } ) \) is non-empty." We thus have an immediate consequence of the Theorem:

Corollary:

Fix an integer \( n \ge 1 \). If \( E_n \) is a finite set, then \( \mathrm{F.P.}(n) \) is true.

A few remarks are in order:

  • The converse to the Corollary is false, so that the statement "\( E_n \) is finite " is strictly stronger than the statement \( \mathrm{F.P.}(n) \). Indeed, (Morton---1992) proves that FPS holds for the case \( n=4 \), and this of course implies \( \mathrm{F.P.}(4) \). On the other hand, (Krumm---2017) proves that the set \( E_4 \) is infinite.
  • According to Hilbert's Irreducibility Theorem, the set \( E_n \) is known to be "thin." Thin sets are small (in a suitable sense coming from algebraic geometry), but may still be infinite. Thus, to prove that \( E_n \) is finite is stronger than what is given by Hilbert Irreducibility.
  • In (Krumm---2019), David Krumm proved that \( E_n \) is finite for \( n \in \{5,6,7,9\} \).

We have finally arrived at the main result of my Ph.D. research:

Theorem:

\( E_n \) is finite for every \( n \) at least \( 10 \).

Taking the above Theorem together with the results of (Krumm---2019), we know that \( E_n \) is finite for every \( n \ge 5 \) except possibly for \( n=8 \). As of this writing, we are not sure what happens for \( n=8 \). In the next few sections, we will briefly outline the methods used for proving the Theorem.

Function Fields and Curves

Fix an integer \( n \ge 1 \), and let \( H \) be any subgroup of our dynatomic Galois group \( G_n \). We will let \( L_n^{H} \) denote the fixed field of \( H \) in \( L_n \) . Then each of the fields \( K \), \( L_n^{H} \), and \( L_n \) is a function field over \( \mathbb{Q} \) . That is, for each \( \mathbb{F} \in \{K, L_n^{H} , L_n\} \):

  • \( \mathbb{F} \) is a finitely generated extension of \( \mathbb{Q} \),
  • the extension \( \mathbb{F} / \mathbb{Q} \) has transendence degree \( 1 \), and
  • \( \mathbb{Q} \) is algebraically closed in \( \mathbb{F} \).

By a well-known equivalence of categories (Theorem 3.1.5.), there are curves \( X \), \( Y \), and \( W \) defined over \( \mathbb{Q} \) corresponding to \( L_n \), \( L_n^{H} \), and \( K \) respectively. Then

  • the curve \( W \) corresponding to \( K = \mathbb{Q} (t) \) is \( \mathbb{P} _{\mathbb{Q} } ^{1} \), the projective line defined over \( \mathbb{Q} \) ;
  • the curve \( Y \) corresponding to \( L_n^{H} \) is the quotient curve \( X_H \equiv X / H \) whose points are \( H \)-orbits under the action of \( H \) on \( X \).
We therefore have equivalent diagrams of function fields and curves over \( \mathbb{Q} \).

Dynatomic Modular Curves

Any curve of the form \( X_H \) is called a dynatomic modular curve . If \( C \) is any curve defined over \( \mathbb{Q} \), we will let \( C(\mathbb{Q} ) \) denote the set of all \( \mathbb{Q} \)-rational points of \( C \) . Then \( \mathbb{Q} \)-rational points on dynatomic modular curves are closely related to exceptional values:

Theorem: (Theorem 3.2.5.)

Let \( c \in \mathbb{Q} -D_n \). Then \( c \in E_n \) if and only if \( c \in \pi_H (X_H (\mathbb{Q} )) \) for some proper subgroup \( H \lneq G_n \).

According to the Theorem, to show that \( E_n \) is finite, it suffices to show that \( X_H (\mathbb{Q}) \) is finite for every proper subgroup \( H \lneq G_n \).

Let \( C \) be any curve defined over \( \mathbb{Q} \). The standard tool for showing that \( C(\mathbb{Q} ) \) is finite is Faltings' Theorem. Let \( g(C) \) denote the genus of \( C \) .

Theorem: (Faltings' Theorem)

Let \( C \) be any curve defined over \( \mathbb{Q} \). If \( g(C) \ge 2 \), then \( C(\mathbb{Q}) \) is finite.

Our strategy then will be to show that for every proper subgroup \( H \lneq G_n \), the genus \( g(X_H) \) of the dynatomic modular curve \( X_H \) is at least \( 2 \). By a standard reduction, we can do even better: it suffices to show that \( g(X_M) \ge 2 \) for every maximal subgroup \( M \lneq G_n \). This is precisely what we prove for \( n \ge 10 \):

Theorem: (Corollary 6.2.15., and Theorem 6.3.5.)

Let \( n \ge 10 \). Then \( g(X_M) \ge 2 \) for every maximal subgroup \( M \) of \( G_n \).

Bounding Genus: a Roadmap

In this final section I will briefly outline the methods used to prove the previous Theorem. I will be intentionally "hand-wavey," but will include ample references to precise statements in my Thesis.

First of all, maximal subgroups \( M \) of \( G_n \cong \left( \mathbb{Z} / n \right)^{ r } \rtimes S_{ r } \) come in two distinct "flavors" according to whether or not they map onto \( S_r \): if \( M \) does map onto \( S_r \), then we call \( M \) a vanilla maximal subgroup , and otherwise we call \( M \) a chocolate maximal subgroup (see Section 6.1). To bound genus for chocolate maximals, we apply a powerful Theorem of Guralnick and Shareshian (Theorem 6.3.2.).

To bound genus for each vanilla maximal \( M \), we use the Riemann-Hurwitz genus formula (Theorem 3.2.4.) which expresses the genus \( g(X_M) \) in terms of the index \( [G_n : M] \) and ramification indices \( e(Q \mid P) \) for various places \( P \) and places \( Q \) lying over \( P \). These ramification indices can be computed (Corollary 2.2.15.) as certain indices \( [{ }^{ g } I : { }^{ g } I \cap M ] \) of \( G_n \)-conjugates of inertia subgroups \( I \le G_n \), and then we use this fact in Section 6.2.2 to compute ramification indices above certain finite places. If we assume \( n \) is at least \( 10 \) and then plug all of this ramification data into the Riemann-Hurwitz formula we get our desired lower bounds on genus for vanilla maximals (Corollary 6.2.15).
